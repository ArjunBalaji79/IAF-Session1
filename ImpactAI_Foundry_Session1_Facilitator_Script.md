# ImpactAI Foundry — Session 1 Facilitator Script
### Discovery Day · Bangalore · June 2026

> **How to use this document.** Each block has a slide cue (what's on screen), facilitator notes (what you say and do), and a timing target. Speaker notes are written to be read aloud or paraphrased — adapt freely. The whole day runs ~8 hours with breaks and lunch. Adjust block lengths to your cohort's energy.

> **A note on tone throughout.** These NGOs are participants we invited, not applicants we screened. Everything you say should treat them as capable organizations choosing to build alongside us. Avoid evaluative language ("you qualified," "we selected you"). Prefer collaborative language ("we're building this together," "you know your work better than anyone").

## Block 2 — Session 1: Prompting & Design (10:00–11:45)

### 2a. Quick Prompt (15 min)

**Slide cue:** "Quick Prompt. Ask AI to solve a problem you're facing at work." Bullets: there's no single right prompt; type anything; revise as many times as you want.

**Facilitator notes:**
- Have everyone open their AI tool of choice (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini — your call on what to standardize).
- Tell them to type a *real* work problem in plain language. No technique yet. The point is to feel the raw experience first.
- Walk the room. Note who's stuck so you can support them later.

### 2b. Table Talk — reflect on the raw attempt (10 min)

**Slide cue:** Four questions.
- What did the model ask you?
- What did it recommend?
- What was unexpected?
- What could be improved?

### 2c. What makes a prompt work better?

**Slide cue:** "What patterns make the prompt work better?" with a chat interface visual.

**Facilitator notes:** Transition from the messy first attempt into structure. This is the teaching core of Session 1.

### 2d. The prompting structure

**Slide cue:** Three numbered blocks.

**01 — Give a Role and Goal**
> "You are a [expert, e.g. a fundraising communications specialist] and your objective is to [goal, e.g. draft donor thank-you emails]."

**02 — List the Steps and Context**
> "To complete this, follow these steps: Step 1… Step 2… Step 3…"

**03 — Describe the Final Product (Output)**
> "When you're done, write your final response as: a summary paragraph, then 3 bullet points for next steps."

### 2e. Re-run with structure (10 min)

**Slide cue:** "Try your prompt again with this new structure." Repeat the three blocks. Note: "You're refining your *prompt* — not trying to perfect the *output*."

**Facilitator notes:** Have them start a *fresh* conversation and re-ask using Role / Steps / Output. The contrast with their raw attempt is the lesson.

### 2f. Table Talk — what changed (10 min)

**Slide cue:** Reflection questions.
- Role & Goal: where did you define the model's expertise or purpose?
- Steps & Context: where did you guide how it works with you?
- Output: where did you specify what "done" looks like?
- Which part was hardest to make explicit, and why?

### 2g. Break (15 min)

**Slide cue:** "Break (15 min)."

### 2h. AI Tool Tips & Tricks

**Slide cue:** Settings/privacy screen and the chat input bar.

**Facilitator notes — cover practically:**
- **Privacy/data controls:** show where to turn off model training on your data. Critical for NGOs handling sensitive beneficiary information.
- **Attachments, web search, tools:** show what the input bar can do.
- Tie this forward to the ethics block: free tools aren't private by default.

## Block 3 — Session 2: Defining AI Problems (11:45–1:00)

### 3a. Ethics first — "you're in control"

**Slide cue:** "AI Ethics. You're in control. Adopt AI in the way that's right for you." Then "We're navigating a new frontier…"

**Facilitator notes:** Place this *before* problem definition deliberately. For mission-driven organizations, establishing the guardrails first earns permission to experiment freely afterward.

**Slide cue — three ethics guidelines:**
1. **Personally Identifiable Information** — free AI tools aren't private. Don't share beneficiary or donor data unless it's a secure platform.
2. **Keep a Human in the Loop** — until you fully trust how a tool behaves, review every output before relying on it.
3. **Customize for Your Community** — out-of-the-box models don't reflect every culture or context. Adapt them to truly serve the people you serve.

> Closing line for the slide: *"…but don't let these get in the way of getting started."*

### 3b. The AI Design Workflow

**Slide cue:** Five-step workflow.
1. Define the Problem (the friction)
2. Describe the Current Workflow
3. Clarify the Output
4. Define Success
5. Prototype, Test, and Iterate

**Facilitator notes:** This is the backbone of the whole program. Tell them they'll return to these five steps in every session.

### 3c. Step 1 — Define the Problem (10 min)

**Slide cue:** The fill-in template.
> *My pain point at work is that I spend time **(specific task)**.*
> *If I could reduce or change this by **(specific improvement)**,*
> *I would gain **(time, quality, or clarity impact)**.*

Prompt them to consider both big challenges (hitting fundraising goals) and small ones (a specific type of email).

**Slide cue — weak vs. strong example (adapt to a local NGO context):**

*Weak (real but not AI-solvable):*
> "My pain point is that I spend too much time trying to raise money. If I could fix this by getting more money, I'd gain greater impact."

*Strong (specific, AI-solvable):*
> "My pain point is that I spend time answering hundreds of repetitive enquiries about our programs each week. If I could automate even half of those replies, I'd gain ~5 hours a week for higher-value work."

**Facilitator notes:** The lesson is specificity. Push gently: "Can AI actually touch this, or is it a resourcing problem in disguise?"

### 3d. How do we know AI can solve it? The 5 Solution Types

**Slide cue:** Five solution types with the "I…" quotes.

| Type | Sounds like | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Answer & Assist** | "I get a lot of questions and can't keep up." | Repetitive constituent questions; new HR policy Q&A |
| **Automate the Admin** | "I keep doing the same task over and over." | Cross-checking spreadsheets; scanning receipts; reformatting data |
| **Create & Communicate** | "I need to write or create something to share." | Blog posts; volunteer training materials; simple websites |
| **Sort & Scan** | "I spend too long reviewing things that follow a pattern." | Grading; ranking volunteer applications |
| **Learn & Decide** | "I want insight to make a smarter choice." | Analyzing survey data; planning an expansion |

**Table Talk (5 min):** Discuss with your table which category each of your problems fits, and why.

### 3e. Steps 2–4 — quick walkthrough

Cover these briefly before lunch so teams can keep thinking:
- **Step 2 — Describe the Current Workflow:** Who does what? When? What relies on what? What information moves it forward? Then map those steps into your prompt's "Steps."
- **Step 3 — Clarify the Output:** What should the result look like? Think how you'd brief a new hire. (An answer, a document, an image, a spreadsheet — be concrete.)
- **Step 4 — Define Success:** Write 3–5 things that would tell you the result is "good." Same standard you'd hold a new employee to.

## Block 4 — Session 3: Prototyping Solutions (2:00–3:45)

**Slide cue:** "Prototyping Your Solution." Steps:
1. Make sure your problem feels specific.
2. (Optional) If stuck, grab a cookbook/template prompt and iterate on that.
3. Iterate on the prompt to build your custom solution.
4. (Optional, advanced) Try a Custom GPT or saved project.

> Goal banner: *"By the end of this session you should have something you can run."*

**Facilitator notes:**
- This is mentor-heavy time. Make sure each of the eight organizations has hands-on support.
- Mid-block **Check-In (15 min):** "What's working? What's challenging?" Surface blockers in the room so others learn from them.
- Run your success criteria from Step 4 as the test: What works? What doesn't? What surprised you?

## Block 5 — Session 4: Refining Solutions (3:45–4:45)

**Slide cue:** "Refining Your Solution." Tips:
- Go back through your instructions and add detail.
- Add constraints, plus good and bad examples.
- Give more context by uploading more **public** materials.
- For advanced cases (API calls, custom code), pair with a tech mentor.

**Facilitator notes:** Reinforce the privacy guardrail — "public materials" is deliberate. No beneficiary data into a tool you haven't vetted.
